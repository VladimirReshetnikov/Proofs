Explanation and Certified Evaluation Strategy for OEIS A198683

Part 1: Understanding OEIS A198683 – Exponentiating the Imaginary Unit with All Parenthesizations
=================================================================================================

What Does OEIS A198683 Count?
-----------------------------

**OEIS A198683** is the sequence that counts, for each positive integer $n$, the number of **distinct complex values** one can obtain by taking $n$ copies of the imaginary unit $i$ and exponentiating them in all possible binary parenthesizations (orderings of exponentiation). In other words, we consider all ways to parenthesize the expression  
i^i^i^ \\cdots ^i \\quad \\text{($n$ times)}  
and **evaluate each using the principal branch of complex exponentiation**[oeis.org](https://oeis.org/A198683#:~:text=Number%20of%20distinct%20values%20taken,value%20of%20the%20exponential%20function). Because exponentiation is _not_ associative, different parenthesizations can yield different results. The sequence $a(n)$ counts how many **distinct** values arise.

For example, for $n=3$ (three $i$’s):

*   The two possible parenthesizations are $(i^i)^i$ and $i^{(i^i)}$.
*   Evaluating with principal branch exponentiation gives two different results[oeis.org](https://oeis.org/A198683#:~:text=a,Pi%2F2%29%20%3D%200.2079):
    *   $(i^i)^i = i^{-1} = \\frac{1}{i} = -i$.
    *   $i^{(i^i)} = i^{0.2079...} \\approx 0.9472... + 0.3208...,i$.
*   Thus $a(3)=2$ distinct values.

For $n=4$ (four $i$’s) there are 5 possible parenthesizations (since the number of full binary trees on $n$ leaves is the Catalan number $C\_{n-1}$). However, these 5 parenthesizations yield only 3 distinct complex values[oeis.org](https://oeis.org/A198683#:~:text=a,i). For instance:

*   $i^((i^i)^i) = ((i^i)^i)^i$ (two different trees give the same value), and
*   $(i^i)^{(i^i)} = (i^{(i^i)})^i$ (another pair that coincides).

The first terms of the sequence are:

*   $a(1)=1$ (just $i$ itself),
*   $a(2)=1$ (only $i^i$, whose principal value is $e^{-{\\pi\\over2}} \\approx 0.2079$[oeis.org](https://oeis.org/A198683#:~:text=a,Pi%2F2%29%20%3D%200.2079)),
*   $a(3)=2$,
*   $a(4)=3$, etc.,

and the sequence continues as $1,1,2,3,7,15,34,77,187,462,1152,\\dots$ for $n=1,2,\\dots,11$[oeis.org](https://oeis.org/A198683#:~:text=13). (The value for $n=12$ remains unconfirmed, as we’ll discuss.) Notably, $a(n)$ grows more slowly than the Catalan number $C\_{n-1}$ because many distinct parenthesizations end up producing the _same_ complex number[oeis.org](https://oeis.org/A198683#:~:text=There%20are%20C%28n,all%20arrangements%20produce%20different%20values)[oeis.org](https://oeis.org/A198683#:~:text=a,i).

Why Is This Problem Mathematically Rich?
----------------------------------------

Several deep mathematical phenomena intersect in this problem:

*   **Non-Associativity of Exponentiation:** Exponentiation is famously not associative. In real arithmetic, one must specify whether $a^{(b^c)}$ or $(a^b)^c$ is intended, since they generally differ. In our scenario, all $n$ bases are the same ($i$), but because exponentiation is non-associative, the $C\_{n-1}$ possible ways to parenthesize $n$ copies of $i$ yield potentially many different outcomes[oeis.org](https://oeis.org/A198683#:~:text=There%20are%20C%28n,all%20arrangements%20produce%20different%20values). This sequence is a natural measure of _how much_ non-associativity we get when exponentiating $i$ repeatedly. Classic results by Guy and Selfridge (1973) studied such “laddered” exponentiations and found that exponentiation is _not_ completely free of coincidences – distinct parenthesizations can sometimes agree by algebraic identities. For example, $(2^{(2^2)}) = ((2^2)^2)$ (both equal 16), a coincidence arising from $2^{2^2} = 2^4 = 16 = 4^2 = (2^2)^2$mathoverflow.net. They proved in general that for any integer base $b\\ge2$ there is at least one coincidence at a certain length (specifically, with $4+b$ copies of $b$)mathoverflow.net. Thus, while exponentiation is largely non-associative, **it isn’t “completely” chaotic** – there are occasional exact collisions in value. The case of the base $i$ exhibits both algebraic coincidences and new types of coincidences due to complex analysis (branch cuts), making it an especially intriguing case.
*   **Catalan Numbers Meet Complex Analysis:** The count of raw parenthesization patterns is given by Catalan numbers (1, 2, 5, 14, 42, … for $n=1,2,3,4,5,\\dots$). If every distinct parenthesization yielded a different value, we’d have $a(n)=C\_{n-1}$. However, for complex exponentiation in the principal branch, many of these expressions coincide or “fold together” into the same value[oeis.org](https://oeis.org/A198683#:~:text=There%20are%20C%28n,all%20arrangements%20produce%20different%20values). The sequence $a(n)$ thus lies somewhere between the extreme of full uniqueness (Catalan) and the opposite extreme (all parenthesizations yielding one value, which _would_ happen if exponentiation were associative). This interplay of **combinatorics** (Catalan structures) with **complex analysis** (exponential principal branch) is rich. The complexity arises because each distinct parenthesization corresponds to a distinct _binary tree_, but evaluating that tree involves choices of logarithms and branch cuts that can cause two different trees to yield the exact same complex number. In essence, _Catalan combinatorics meets the topology of the complex exponential function._ Understanding $a(n)$ requires analyzing how the combinatorial structure of the exponentiation tree influences the resulting complex number.
*   **Branch Cuts and Principal Values:** The “^” operation here is defined via the **principal value** of complex exponentiation. By definition, $a^b = \\exp(b \\Log(a))$ where $\\Log(a)$ is the principal branch of the complex logarithm. The principal log $\\Log(z)$ is single-valued and typically defined with a branch cut along the negative real axis, taking arguments $\\Arg(z)\\in(-\\pi,\\pi\]$. This choice introduces discontinuities and asymmetries: complex exponentiation is no longer periodic or associative in a simple way. **Small changes in an exponent can lead to large jumps** if those changes cross a branch cut. In our context, as we build up towers like $i^{(i^{(i^i)})}$, the intermediate results may wander around the complex plane and occasionally cross the branch cut, drastically altering subsequent values. Two different parenthesizations can yield the same value _if one sequence of exponentiations accumulates a $2\\pi k$ difference in argument at some stage_, effectively “catching up” with another sequence. A vivid example from the OEIS comments: if $Y = ((i^i)^i)^i$, one can show  
     $(i^i)^Y / (i^Y)^i = \exp(-2\pi)$   
    [oeis.org](https://oeis.org/A198683#:~:text=Note%20that%20if%20a%20is,Sloane%2C%20Nov%2028%202015). This indicates that $(i^i)^Y$ and $(i^Y)^i$ differ by the factor $e^{-2\\pi}$, so the expected identity $(i^i)^Y = i^{Y\\cdot i}$ _fails by a transcendental factor_. Such branch cut-induced deviations are responsible for many of the distinct outcomes. The principal branch forces each exponentiation step to make a choice of log value (usually the one with imaginary part in $(-\\pi,\\pi\]$), and different parenthesizations can lead to different cumulative choices of $2\\pi$ multiples. Tracking these **“wraparound” adjustments** is at the heart of why $a(n)$ grows smaller than $C\_{n-1}$.
*   **Extreme Growth of Magnitudes:** As $n$ increases, the typical _magnitude_ of values encountered can explode at an absurd rate. Even for moderate $n$, some parenthesizations yield towers of exponentials so large that their magnitudes defy intuition. For example, at $n=10$ one particular way of parenthesizing gives an intermediate exponent $C \\approx -6.795047376\\times 10^{34} - i,6.044219499\\times10^{34}$ (a huge complex number). Then at $n=11$, we encounter $i^C$, whose magnitude is astronomically large:  
     $|i^C| = \exp\!\Big(\Re(C)\cdot \Re(\Log(i)) - \Im(C)\cdot \Im(\Log(i))\Big).$   
    Since $\\Log(i) = i\\pi/2$, this simplifies to $|i^C| = \\exp!\\big((-\\pi/2)\\Im(C)\\big)$. With $\\Im(C)\\approx -6.04\\times10^{34}$, we get  
     $|i^C| = \exp\!\Big((-\pi/2)\cdot(-6.0442\times10^{34})\Big) \approx 4.1007\times 10^{4.1233\times10^{33}},$   
    an **inconceivably large** number (tower-of-ten type size)[oeis.org](https://oeis.org/A198683#:~:text=At%20n%3D10%2C%20the%20expression%20i,Schoenfield%2C%20Nov%2021%202015). Such huge magnitudes arise routinely by $n=11$, and by $n=12$ some expressions produce numbers so large that representing or distinguishing them from each other becomes a Herculean task. The vast range of magnitudes means naive numeric computation will overflow or lose precision. It also means that two expressions might produce values that are _both_ astronomically large but differ by a subtle factor (like $e^{-2\\pi}$ in the earlier example) – detecting such a small ratio between two huge values demands extreme precision. This **extreme growth** is a major obstacle in enumerating distinct values: we must be sure that two results aren’t equal, even when both are far outside any normal computational range.
*   **Subtleties of Equality (Periodicities and Mod 4 Behavior):** When do two different exponentiation outcomes actually coincide? For real bases, one known upper bound on distinct outcomes was given by sequence A000081 (the count of rooted trees)[oeis.org](https://oeis.org/A198683#:~:text=Note%20that%20if%20a%20is,Sloane%2C%20Nov%2028%202015), relying on identities like $(x^y)^z=(x^z)^y$ that hold for positive reals. However, those identities break down in the complex principal branch[oeis.org](https://oeis.org/A198683#:~:text=Note%20that%20if%20a%20is,Sloane%2C%20Nov%2028%202015). In the specific case of base $i$, there is a particular periodicity to exploit: _raising $i$ to a power has an inherent 4-cycle._ In fact, $i^z = i^w$ if and only if $z-w$ is an integer multiple of 4 (and with zero imaginary part)[oeis.org](https://oeis.org/A198683#:~:text=Note%20that%20if%20a%20is,Sloane%2C%20Nov%2028%202015). This comes from the fact that $i^{4}=1$ and the principal branch takes $\\Log(i)=i\\pi/2$. More explicitly,  
     $i^z = \exp(z\,\Log(i)) = \exp(z\,i\pi/2).$   
    For this to equal $i^w = \\exp(w,i\\pi/2)$, we require $(z-w),i\\pi/2 = 2\\pi i k$ for some integer $k$. Cancelling $i\\pi/2$ gives $z-w = 4k$ (a real integer multiple of 4). In simpler terms, two exponentiation results will coincide if one exponent differs from the other by an exact multiple of 4. In practice, this means that if we can express the outcome of any parenthesized tower as $i^Z$ (for some complex exponent $Z$), then all equality testing reduces to checking whether pairs of these exponents differ by $4\\mathbb{Z}$ (and have exactly zero imaginary difference). This **“mod 4” equivalence** is a crucial simplification: rather than comparing two enormous complex numbers directly, we compare their exponents in a transformed space where the periodicity is linear. However, determining those exponents $Z$ to high enough precision and certifying the “multiple of 4” condition is itself non-trivial, especially when $Z$ might be astronomically large or have tiny imaginary parts that must be distinguished from 0.

In summary, the problem of computing $a(n)$ is rich because it sits at a crossroads of combinatorics, algebra, and analysis. We see **associative laws failing** in interesting ways, **combinatorial explosion tempered by complex periodicity**, and the need for **careful analytic handling of huge numbers and tiny differences**.

Why Is Computing $a(12)$ So Difficult?
--------------------------------------

By $n=12$, the situation has escalated dramatically on all fronts:

*   **Explosion of Cases:** There are $C\_{11} = 58786$ possible parenthesizations for 12 items (Catalan number for $n-1=11$). That’s nearly 60k distinct exponentiation orders to evaluate and compare. The sequence value $a(12)$ will be the count of distinct outcomes among those. For smaller $n$, one could brute-force evaluate each and deduplicate (indeed, the OEIS entry provides a brute-force Mathematica snippet up to $n=10$[oeis.org](https://oeis.org/A198683#:~:text=MATHEMATICA)). But at $n=12$, evaluating $58k$ huge towers reliably is a massive challenge.
*   **Astronomical Intermediate Values:** As mentioned, even by $n=11$ some intermediate results exceed $10^{10^{33}}$ in magnitude[oeis.org](https://oeis.org/A198683#:~:text=At%20n%3D10%2C%20the%20expression%20i,Schoenfield%2C%20Nov%2021%202015). By $n=12$, certain exponentials will be stacks one level taller, potentially yielding numbers of a size that can hardly be written down, let alone compared directly. Standard floating-point arithmetic is utterly insufficient here; even arbitrary-precision arithmetic strains when exponents themselves have dozens of digits. The sheer size of numbers means that any rounding error or imprecision can lead to a completely wrong conclusion (for example, an extremely large magnitude might overflow or get approximated as $\\infty$ in a computer algebra system, losing the subtle distinctions needed to tell two values apart). Thus, a successful computation requires a custom approach to represent enormous numbers symbolically or with rigorous error bounds.
*   **Precision to Track Branch Cuts:** Because we insist on **principal branch exponentiation at every step**, we must carefully determine the argument of each intermediate result and ensure it stays within $(-\\pi,\\pi\]$. When exponentiating complex numbers, a tiny change in an intermediate value can decide whether the next logarithm “wraps around” by $2\\pi$ or not. Near the branch cut (e.g. an argument near $+\\pi$ or $-\\pi$), finite precision calculations might _mis-classify_ which side of the cut the number falls on. For example, an argument of $+3.14159$ vs $-3.14159$ (radians) is effectively the same direction in the plane (nearly $\\pi$), but principal branch conventions would take one as $\\pi$ (just within the cut) and the other as $-3.14159$ (just under $\\pi$ from the negative side). If an algorithm isn’t precise enough, it might incorrectly apply or miss a $2\\pi$ adjustment (what we can call a **wrap correction**). By $n=12$, some values will have arguments exceedingly close to the branch cut boundaries, meaning we need **extreme precision and careful error tracking** to correctly decide when to add/subtract $2\\pi$. A single misstep in branch cut handling could cause two expressions that should yield the same value to appear different (or vice versa). Thus, computing $a(12)$ requires **rigorously tracking the principal argument choices** at each exponentiation.
*   **Cascading Error and Wrap Propagation:** Each exponentiation in the tower can amplify errors dramatically. If we are off by even a tiny $\\epsilon$ in an exponent, the next level $i^z$ will involve $\\exp(z , i\\pi/2)$, where the imaginary part of $z$ multiplies $\\pi/2$ in the exponent of an exponential. A small uncertainty in $\\Im(z)$ can turn into huge multiplicative uncertainty in the magnitude after exponentiation (since $e^{-\\Im(z)\\pi/2}$ gives the magnitude). Moreover, any missed $2\\pi k$ in the argument will propagate upward. For instance, if one intermediate angle should have been say $-3.1416$ (just slightly past $-\\pi$) but was mis-recorded as $+3.1416$, the next exponentiation might effectively be off by a factor of $e^{\\pm2\\pi}$ (about 535.5) in the result! The effect snowballs up the tower. Therefore, to get _correct, provable_ results at $n=12$, one needs to maintain **tight error intervals** for both magnitude ($\\rho$) and argument ($\\theta$) of every intermediate complex number. If an interval straddles a critical decision boundary (like $-\\pi$ vs $\\pi$), the computation must refine further (increase precision or use exact rational bounds) until the decision is unambiguous. This is far beyond a straightforward numerical calculation; it borders on a **proof** for each equality/inequality of outcomes.
*   **Equality Detection and Deduplication:** Finally, even after computing all outcomes with high precision, one must determine which ones are equal. As noted, for base $i$ any equality means two results can be written as $i^{z\_1}$ and $i^{z\_2}$ with $z\_1 - z\_2 = 4k$ (an integer multiple of 4)[oeis.org](https://oeis.org/A198683#:~:text=Note%20that%20if%20a%20is,Sloane%2C%20Nov%2028%202015). However, verifying this in practice for $n=12$ requires high confidence in the values of $z\_1$ and $z\_2$. If two results are extremely close, one must ascertain _why_ they are close: either they are mathematically equal or they are just very large numbers that ended up almost the same by coincidence (within numerical error). There’s no room for guessing; we need **proof-level certainty**. This means implementing robust checks: e.g. subtract $z\_2$ from $z\_1$, reduce the real part modulo 4, and confirm the difference is _exactly_ $0$ (with an error interval that does not include any other integer) and that the imaginary part of the difference is provably $0$. Achieving such certification might require calculating with hundreds or thousands of digits of precision or using interval arithmetic that guarantees containment of the true value. The endgame is to be absolutely sure that, say, $a(12)$ is one specific integer (the number of distinct equivalence classes), and not one less or more due to an unnoticed duplication.

All these factors make $a(12)$ a hard nut to crack. In fact, as of the last update, it remains _unconfirmed_. The OEIS entry notes that preliminary computations by contributors yielded two candidates, 2919 or 2926, for $a(12)$[oeis.org](https://oeis.org/A198683#:~:text=a,del%20Arte%2C%20Nov%2017%202011). This is a call to go beyond heuristic or numerical computation and develop a _rigorous computational proof_.

Part 2: A Proposed Rigorous Approach to Certifying $a(12)$
==========================================================

To determine $a(12)$ with certainty, we need a strategy that can handle the huge numbers and delicate equivalences described above. Here we outline a computational approach designed for **rigor** over efficiency – one that uses interval arithmetic and symbolic reasoning to ensure no distinct value is mistaken for another (or vice versa).

1\. **Interval-Based Polar Representation of Values**
-----------------------------------------------------

Every intermediate result of our exponentiations can be represented in **polar form** as $(\\rho, \\theta)$ – where $\\rho$ is the magnitude (a non-negative real) and $\\theta$ is the argument (angle) in radians. Rather than storing a complex number as a single floating-point, we will store **interval bounds** for $\\rho$ and $\\theta$, ideally as rational numbers or high-precision dyadic fractions. This means for each value we carry an interval $\[\\rho\_{\\min}, \\rho\_{\\max}\]$ that provably contains the true magnitude, and an interval $\[\\theta\_{\\min}, \\theta\_{\\max}\]$ for the true argument. The initial value $i$ would be represented as $\\rho=1$ exactly, $\\theta=\\pi/2$ exactly (since $i = e^{i\\pi/2}$).

Using interval arithmetic ensures that **round-off and truncation errors are accounted for** at every step. If we perform all arithmetic in a way that tracks error (for example, using a library like Arb (which does ball arithmetic) or using rational bounds by converting floating results to safe upper/lower rationals), we can guarantee that the true value lies in the computed interval. Initially, these intervals can be extremely tight (for $i$, exactly $\[1,1\]$ and $\[\\pi/2,\\pi/2\]$). As we perform operations, the intervals may grow slightly to accommodate uncertainty, but we will control that growth by using high precision and refinement.

2\. **Propagating Through the Exponentiation Trees**
----------------------------------------------------

We will generate all binary exponentiation expressions for $n=12$. Rather than evaluate each completely independently, we can do it recursively (dynamic programming style). For example, any parenthesization of 12 $i$’s can be seen as $(\\text{expression of $k$ $i$’s})^{(\\text{expression of $n-k$ $i$’s})}$ for some split $1 \\le k < n$. We can build up from smaller $n$ to larger. In fact, the Mathematica code in the OEIS entry does exactly this, but using naive numeric union[oeis.org](https://oeis.org/A198683#:~:text=MATHEMATICA). We will do similarly, but each “value” will be an interval pair $(\\rho,\\theta)$ rather than a single approximate complex.

**Exponentiation rule:** Suppose we have already computed all possible distinct outcomes for $k$ $i$’s and for $n-k$ $i$’s, represented as sets of $(\\rho,\\theta)$ intervals. To combine a left result $L=(\\rho\_L, \\theta\_L)$ raised to a right result $R=(\\rho\_R,\\theta\_R)$, we do the following:

*   **Compute $\\Log(L)$:** We need the principal log of the complex number represented by $L$. If $L = \\rho\_L e^{i\\theta\_L}$ (with $\\theta\_L$ already kept in principal range $(-\\pi,\\pi\]$), then  
     $\Log(L) = \ln(\rho_L) + i \theta_L,$   
    where $\\ln(\\rho\_L)$ is the natural log of the magnitude (a real number) and $\\theta\_L$ is already the principal argument. We use our interval for $\\rho\_L$ to produce an interval for $\\ln(\\rho\_L)$ (using, say, monotonicity of log to bound it). We already have an interval for $\\theta\_L$. Thus we get an interval representation for $\\Log(L)$ as a complex rectangle: $\[\\ln(\\rho\_L)_{\\min}, \\ln(\\rho\_L)_{\\max}\] + i\[\\theta\_L\_{\\min},\\theta\_L\_{\\max}\]$.
*   **Form the exponent $E = R \\cdot \\Log(L)$:** The new exponent is the product of the complex number represented by $R$ and the log of $L$. Write $R = \\rho\_R e^{i\\theta\_R}$. In rectangular form, $R$ is approximately $\\rho\_R \\cos\\theta\_R + i \\rho\_R \\sin\\theta\_R$. We have intervals for $\\rho\_R$ and $\\theta\_R$ as well. We then multiply the two complex intervals $R$ and $\\Log(L)$. This is done by considering all combinations of bounds to get an over-approximation (interval) for the product. We must be careful: complex interval multiplication can enlarge the result interval, but we can mitigate by keeping precision high. The result of this step is an interval for the complex exponent $E = R \\cdot \\Log(L)$. Call that $E = U + iV$ (with $U$, $V$ intervals for real and imaginary parts respectively).
*   **Exponentiate to get the new value:** The new value $N = L^R = \\exp(E)$ in principal value terms. We compute $\\exp(E)$ by using the formula $\\exp(U + iV) = \\exp(U)\\cdot (\\cos V + i\\sin V)$. Now, $U$ is an interval for the real part of $E$, and $V$ for the imaginary part. We can get an interval for $\\exp(U)$ (which will be one-sided: $\[\\exp(U\_{\\min}), \\exp(U\_{\\max})\]$ since $\\exp$ is monotonic on reals). For the $\\cos V$ and $\\sin V$ factors, we note that $V$ (the imaginary part of $E$) corresponds to the _argument_ of the new value $N$. Here is a **critical point:** We must reduce $V$ modulo $2\\pi$ to bring it into the principal branch range for the argument of $N$. However, since we constructed $E$ from principal logs, $V$ should already lie within a range that corresponds to the principal outcome. But $V$ might span more than $2\\pi$ if our intervals are very wide (which we should try to avoid). Realistically, with careful refinement at each step, $V$’s interval should not cover more than a range of size $2\\pi$ if the previous steps were principal. But if it does, we need to **split** the interval or refine further, because an interval for $V$ that is larger than $2\\pi$ would straddle multiple possible wraps (and we’d lose information about the correct branch).
    Assuming $V$ is reasonably narrow (say $V\_{\\min},V\_{\\max}$ are within a $2\\pi$ span), we then define the new magnitude and angle:
    *   $\\rho\_N = \\exp(U)$ (we have an interval for this).
    *   $\\theta\_N = V \\mod (-\\pi,\\pi\]$. We ensure the interval $\[V\_{\\min},V\_{\\max}\]$ lies entirely inside a $2\\pi$ range. If it crosses the $-\\pi$/$\\pi$ boundary, this means our result $N$ lies _across_ the branch cut. In such a case, we should refine the computation of $E$ with higher precision or subdivide the range for $R$ or $L$ until we can determine on which side of the cut $N$ falls. Ultimately, we want $\\theta\_N$ as an interval within $(-\\pi,\\pi\]$. If $V$ interval is, for example, $\[3.10, 3.18\]$ (which crosses $\\pi \\approx 3.1416$), we’d detect that it overlaps the boundary $\\pi$. We’d then likely split that interval or refine, because the true $\\theta$ might be near $\\pi$ from either side. In worst case, if $N$ is exactly on the branch cut (e.g. a negative real), its argument is exactly $\\pi$ (principal) – we can allow $\\theta=\\pi$ as an endpoint if needed, but we must be consistent (principal branch convention typically includes $\\pi$ and excludes $-\\pi$). The algorithm must handle that carefully, perhaps by always rounding $\\theta$ intervals outward so that if $\\pi$ is a possible value, one interval endpoint is $\\pi$.
    At the end of this exponentiation, we have a new interval $(\\rho\_N,\\theta\_N)$ for the result $N = L^R$.
*   **Maintain Precision and Reduce Overlap:** At each arithmetic step, we should monitor the width of the intervals. If they become too wide (relative to the tolerance needed to distinguish values), we increase the precision of computation and redo that step. For example, if two results’ $\\theta$ intervals overlap significantly, we might need more precision to shrink them, or else we can’t tell if they might coincide. We especially refine when an interval straddles critical values like 0, $\\pi$, or when two different results’ intervals start to overlap in a way that could indicate a possible equality.

By recursively applying the above, we can compute a set of interval-represented results for $n=12$. To avoid combinatorial explosion in memory, we will **merge identical results** on the fly: whenever we compute a new result $N$, we compare it against the list of results we already have for that $n$. “Compare” here means check if the intervals $(\\rho\_N,\\theta\_N)$ and, say, $(\\rho\_{M},\\theta\_{M})$ for an existing result $M$ **definitely do not overlap** in a way that could permit equality, or if they might represent the same complex value. If the intervals are disjoint (e.g. $M$’s magnitude range does not even overlap $N$’s, or the argument ranges are far apart), then $M$ and $N$ are certainly different values. If they do overlap tightly, we cannot conclude distinctness yet – we must consider a higher-precision calculation or a direct equality test via exponents (next section). In practice, we might maintain a canonical representative for each distinct value found so far and use interval overlap as a quick filter to identify potential duplicates.

Throughout this propagation, we also carry along the **branch cut decisions** we made (the $2\\pi$ adjustments). Essentially, for each exponentiation, we could record if we had to subtract $2\\pi$ or not to keep $\\theta$ in range. This record acts as a _witness_ to how each value was obtained in the principal branch. If two different parenthesizations end up with the same sequence of branch adjustments, they are likely to coincide (though it’s possible to coincide even with different adjustment histories, if they differ by a full $4k$ cycle net effect).

3\. **Equality Checking via $i^z$ Exponent Difference**
-------------------------------------------------------

Once we have all candidate results for $n=12$ as interval pairs, we need to rigorously identify which are equal. As discussed, any complex number (except 0) can be expressed in the form $i^Z$ for some complex exponent $Z$. In fact, given our polar form $N = \\rho e^{i\\theta}$, solving $N = i^Z$ is straightforward on principal branches:

*   We require $i^Z = \\exp(Z \\cdot \\Log(i)) = \\exp(Z \\cdot (i\\pi/2)) = \\rho e^{i\\theta}$.
*   So $Z \\cdot (i\\pi/2) = \\Log(N) = \\ln(\\rho) + i\\theta$ (with $\\theta$ already principal for $N$).
*   This implies  
     $Z = \frac{2}{\pi} (\,-\,i\ln(\rho) + \theta\,).$   
    (Because multiplying both sides by $-2i/\\pi$: $Z = -\\frac{2i}{\\pi}(\\ln \\rho + i\\theta) = \\frac{2\\theta}{\\pi} + i\\left(-\\frac{2\\ln\\rho}{\\pi}\\right)$.)

So if $N=(\\rho,\\theta)$, one choice for $Z$ is  
 $Z = \frac{2\theta}{\pi} - i\frac{2\ln(\rho)}{\pi}.$   
We will compute such a $Z$ (with interval arithmetic) for each distinct value candidate. We also **reduce** the real part of $Z$ into the interval $\[0,4)$ by subtracting an appropriate integer if necessary (because $i^Z$ has $4$-periodicity in the real part of $Z$). We now have a canonical representation of each value as $i^Z$ where $\\Re(Z)\\in\[0,4)$.

To check if two results $N\_1 = i^{Z\_1}$ and $N\_2 = i^{Z\_2}$ are equal, we need to check if $Z\_1 - Z\_2$ is an integer multiple of 4 (and has zero imaginary part). In practice, we compute the difference $\\Delta Z = Z\_1 - Z\_2$. This difference is itself an interval (complex interval). Now:

*   We examine the imaginary part of $\\Delta Z$. This corresponds to $-\\frac{2}{\\pi}(\\ln(\\rho\_1) - \\ln(\\rho\_2))$. If $N\_1$ and $N\_2$ are truly equal, this imaginary part should be exactly zero (their magnitudes must be equal). We check if $0$ lies in the interval for $\\Im(\\Delta Z)$. If that interval is, say, $\[0.0, 1.2\\times10^{-50}\]$ (something tiny containing 0), we suspect they _might_ be equal in magnitude, but we need to shrink that uncertainty with higher precision. If the interval for $\\Im(\\Delta Z)$ does not include 0 (e.g. it’s $\[0.123, 0.124\]$ or $\[-0.05,-0.02\]$), then the magnitudes differ and the numbers are definitely distinct. In case the interval does straddle 0, we increase precision to narrow it. Ultimately, for equality candidates we want to show $\\Im(\\Delta Z)=0$ exactly, which in our arithmetic will appear as the interval collapsing to \[0,0\] or something within a tolerance we can attribute to rounding. Achieving that might involve extremely high precision logarithms of $\\rho\_1$ and $\\rho\_2$ to see that they are exactly equal. Often, if two distinct parenthesizations yield the same number, there will be a theoretical reason (like a known identity) that ensures the magnitudes are equal; our algorithm’s job is to identify that by narrowing the difference.
*   Next, we look at the **real part** of $\\Delta Z$. This should be an integer (multiplying by 1, since we normalized in \[0,4) range for each $Z$) if they are equal. More precisely, $\\Re(Z\_1) - \\Re(Z\_2)$ should be $4k$ for some integer $k$ (and we already adjusted each to \[0,4), so typically it would be exactly $0$ if we chose the same branch for both). If our earlier normalization put $\\Re(Z)$ in \[0,4) for each, then equal numbers should yield either the same $\\Re(Z)$ or one at 0 and one at 4 which represent the same angle (we might keep 4 exclusive to avoid double cover). We will ensure consistency in that normalization. So we check $\\Re(\\Delta Z)$: if $N\_1$ and $N\_2$ are equal, $\\Re(\\Delta Z)$ should be an integer multiple of 4. Given both are in \[0,4), the only possibilities are 0 or ±4. ±4 would occur if one had exactly 0 and the other 4, which is essentially the same angle; we can consider that equal too. In practice, because we constrain to \[0,4), we expect $\\Re(\\Delta Z)=0$ for equal numbers. So we check if the interval for $\\Re(\\Delta Z)$ contains 0 (or 4). If it’s clearly away from those values, they’re distinct. If it’s straddling, again we refine precision.

In essence, this step reduces equality checking to a combination of **integer detection** (is a certain real number essentially an integer?) and **zero detection** (is a small imaginary interval containing 0 or not?). Both are classic uses of interval arithmetic in number verification – one can gradually increase precision until either the interval is so small that it excludes the property (e.g. does not contain any integer), or it collapses onto the property (contains exactly one integer, etc.).

For example, if two expressions purportedly yield the same value, we might find $\\Re(Z\_1)\\approx 2.0000000000000000$ and $\\Re(Z\_2)\\approx 1.9999999999999998$ after some precision – the difference $\\approx 2\\times10^{-16}$, well within error. We’d crank up the precision (say from 64-bit to 500-bit) and recompute. If indeed they are equal, the difference should become exactly 0 when fully resolved (or arbitrarily small and eventually bounded within an interval containing 0 but no other integers). If they are not equal, at high precision the difference will stabilize to a non-zero value. This kind of **interval bisection and refinement** continues until all ambiguities are resolved.

4\. **Managing Error Bounds and Edge Cases**
--------------------------------------------

To actually implement the above for $n=12$, a careful strategy is required:

*   We will likely need **adaptive precision**: start with, say, 200 or 300 bits of precision for arbitrary real numbers (to handle the logs and exponents), and increase as needed. The explosion in magnitude suggests we might need thousands of bits to accurately differentiate values.
*   We should use **rational approximations** wherever possible for critical constants. Note that many intermediate numbers are not arbitrary: for instance, $i^i = e^{-\\pi/2}$ exactly, which is transcendental but we know its value to high precision via $\\pi$. We might keep $\\pi$ symbolic to some extent. If needed, we can use a rational interval for $\\pi$ (with known enclosures) to ensure no slip in branch decisions.
*   **Edge case: near the branch cut.** Suppose an expression yields a value extremely close to $-1$ (which has an argument of $\\pi$). One parenthesization might give an argument just a hair below $\\pi$, another just at $\\pi$ – these could actually be the same value if one hit exactly $-1$ and the other hit the same $-1$ from the other side. We must not count them as distinct. Our interval method would show one value’s $\\theta$ interval perhaps as $\[3.1415, 3.1416\]$ and another’s as $\[-3.1416,-3.1415\]$ (if one got interpreted as slightly over $\\pi$ and wrapped to -$\\pi$). These intervals don’t overlap, which naively would suggest distinct values, but in truth both are $-1$ if they were exact $\\pi$. This scenario suggests we should treat an interval containing $\\pi$ at an endpoint with special care – recognizing that $\\theta = \\pi$ and $\\theta = -\\pi$ represent the same direction (principal branch chooses $\\pi$). Our algorithm can handle this by consistently using $\\pi$ (not $-\\pi$) for negative reals. If a $\\theta$ interval happens to cross from just below $\\pi$ to just above $-\\pi$, it’s a sign of a value near $-1$. We would enforce that if $\\rho$ interval contains 1 and $\\theta$ spans the cut, we refine to see if it’s exactly $1$ and $\\pi$. In short, we include logic for when a point lies exactly on the cut (negative real axis): in that case, set $\\theta=\\pi$ exactly and note it.
*   **Interval narrowing:** At times, the algorithm may produce an interval that’s too conservative. For instance, multiplying two broad intervals could yield an interval much larger than the actual uncertainty. We can employ _bisection_: split the interval for one operand and compute two separate outcomes, thereby tightening the range. This is feasible because even though there are many parenthesizations, the structure is fixed; most values will cluster well away from problematic boundaries, and only a few will need intense refinement. We can focus computational effort on those borderline cases where potential equalities or branch ambiguities occur. This targeted refinement prevents an explosion of work on every single case.
*   **Use of Known Identities:** As a cross-check, we have some analytical understanding. For example, we know from lower $n$ results that certain forms coincide (the OEIS examples at $n=4$ gave explicit equalities). If at $n=12$ we can guess certain patterns that might equal, we could insert those as “hints” or checks in our program to verify. (For instance, if some sub-expression yields an exponent that is exactly an integer, that might simplify things; or if a substructure repeats, maybe its values repeat.) Guy and Selfridge’s general formula might predict specific coincidences that we can directly confirm (like any that involve $(i^i)^i$ type sub-towers yielding something like $e^{\\pi/2}$ as we saw). While the algorithm doesn’t rely on these, it’s good to keep them in mind to ensure our results match known theory.

In summary, the proposed approach treats the problem almost like a **computer-assisted proof**. We rigorously enclose each value in a guaranteed safe interval and only declare two values distinct if their intervals provably do not overlap in the “$i^Z$ mod 4” sense outlined above. Conversely, we only declare them equal if we can definitively show $Z\_1 - Z\_2 = 4k$ with zero uncertainty. This approach replaces naive heuristic guessing (e.g. assuming floating-point equality up to some tolerance) with **guaranteed correctness**, at the cost of heavy computation.
