Here’s what **A198683** is about, why it’s such a fun rabbit‑hole, and why pushing it from $n=11$ to $n=12$ is unexpectedly hard.

---

## What is being counted?

Fix the base $i=\sqrt{-1}$. For each $n\ge 1$, form **all** binary parenthesizations of

$$
\underbrace{i^{i^{\cdot^{\cdot^{i}}}}}_{n\text{ copies of }i}
$$

and evaluate every one **using the principal value of complex powers**:

$$
a^b \stackrel{\text{def}}{=} \exp\!\big(b\,\Log a\big),\qquad 
\Log a=\ln|a|+i\,\Arg(a),\quad \Arg\in(-\pi,\pi].
$$

Then $a(n)$ is the number of **distinct** complex values obtained. (There are $C_{n-1}$ parenthesizations, with $C_k$ the $k$-th Catalan number, but collisions occur: different trees can evaluate to the same complex number.) ([OEIS][1], [Wikipedia][2])

Because $\Log i = i\pi/2$, one particularly convenient identity is

$$
i^z = \exp\!\left(\frac{i\pi}{2}\,z\right),
$$

so $|i^z|=\exp\!\big(-\tfrac{\pi}{2}\operatorname{Im} z\big)$ and $\arg(i^z)\equiv \tfrac{\pi}{2}\operatorname{Re} z \pmod{2\pi}$. ([personalpages.manchester.ac.uk][3])

OEIS gives the first values

$$
a(1),\dots,a(11)=1,1,2,3,7,15,34,77,187,462,1152,
$$

with small‑$n$ examples worked out explicitly (e.g., at $n=4$ five bracketings collapse to only three distinct values). ([OEIS][1])

## Why is this fascinating?

1. **Catalan combinatorics meets complex analysis.**
   Each parenthesization is a binary tree; you get $C_{n-1}$ such trees. But evaluation uses the *principal* branch of $\Log$, which is discontinuous across the negative real axis. That discontinuity injects arithmetic “carry digits” of size $2\pi$ at every internal node—turning a purely combinatorial problem into a delicate analytic one. ([OEIS][1], [Wikipedia][4])

2. **Exponentiation is *not* associative with principal values.**
   In the reals, $x>0$ implies $(x^y)^z=x^{yz}=(x^z)^y$. With principal complex powers this breaks: if $Y=((i^i)^i)^i$, then

   $$
   \frac{(i^i)^Y}{(i^Y)^i}=\exp(-2\pi)\neq 1,
   $$

   a vivid, exact instance of nonassociativity caused by branch corrections. ([OEIS][1])

3. **Occasional “islands of associativity.”**
   Sometimes intermediate values happen to be *positive real*, in which case the real‑power laws do hold locally and different trees collapse. Guy–Selfridge’s classic paper explains the real‑base story and its tight link to rooted trees (A000081) and why many parenthesizations nonetheless yield the same number; in the complex principal‑value world we see analogous—but more erratic—collapses.&#x20;

4. **Astronomical scales appear quickly.**
   By $n=10$ one specific bracketing already yields

   $$
   C\approx -6.795\times10^{34}-i\,6.044\times 10^{34},
   $$

   so at $n=11$, $i^C=\exp\!\big(\tfrac{i\pi}{2}C\big)$ has modulus about $e^{(\pi/2)\,\!6.044\times10^{34}}\approx 10^{4.1\times 10^{34}}$. You will never store that number directly; you must reason with logs and arguments only. ([OEIS][1])

5. **A periodicity that creates subtle collisions.**
   Since $i^{z+4}=i^z$, any two top‑level exponents that differ by an integer multiple of $4$ yield exactly the same final value—so detecting equality reduces to a *mod 4* question on gigantic complex exponents. That sounds simple, but it isn’t (see below).

## A precise picture of what goes wrong (and right)

Write every nonzero complex number uniquely as $x=\exp(\rho+i\theta)$ with $\theta\in(-\pi,\pi]$. If $y=a+ib$, then

$$
x^y=\exp\!\Big((a\rho-b\theta)+i(a\theta+b\rho)\Big).
$$

To *feed this into the next exponentiation* one needs $\Log(x^y)$, and here the principal branch forces a wrap:

$$
\Log(x^y)=(a\rho-b\theta) + i\Big(\underbrace{a\theta+b\rho}_{\text{raw phase}} - 2\pi k\Big),
$$

where the integer $k$ is chosen so that the imaginary part lands in $(-\,\pi,\pi]$. These integers $k$ (one at **every internal node** of every tree) are the exact reason the usual laws fail and different trees diverge: moving parentheses changes the *place* you apply “mod $2\pi$,” and that propagates to the top. The ratio

$$
\frac{(x^y)^z}{(x^z)^y}
=\exp\!\Big(-2\pi i\,(kz-\ell y)\Big)
$$

exhibits this explicitly (here $k,\ell\in\mathbb Z$ are the wraps chosen in $\Log(x^y)$, $\Log(x^z)$). For the concrete $x=i$ example in the OEIS note this ratio evaluates to $\exp(-2\pi)$. ([OEIS][1])

## Why $a(12)$ is so hard to pin down exactly

By $n=12$ there are $C_{11}=58{,}786$ parenthesizations to evaluate, but the challenge is *not* mere volume—it’s certification. Several independent difficulties pile up:

1. **Wild numerical scales make naive evaluation impossible.**
   Already at $n=11$ some intermediate values have moduli like $10^{10^{34}}$. Any implementation that tries to construct these numbers in floating‑point (even “arbitrary precision” reals) will overflow/underflow. You must carry only $(\rho,\theta)$ pairs and never form $e^\rho$ when $\rho$ is huge—otherwise even basic deduplication fails. This is exactly what the OEIS comment about the $n=10\to 11$ step warns about. ([OEIS][1])

2. **Branch‑cut decisions must be *proved*, not guessed.**
   At each internal node you must choose the unique integer $k$ so that the new phase lies in $(-\,\pi,\pi]$. When the raw phase $a\theta+b\rho$ is enormous (think $10^{34}$) you are taking it **mod $2\pi$**. That requires knowing the value to *absolute* error $<\pi$ just to pick the right $k$; and if you are unlucky and the phase lies extremely close to a boundary (near $\pm\pi$), you need thousands of extra bits to certify you didn’t round to the wrong side. A single off‑by‑$2\pi$ at one interior node can flip a value and create (or destroy) a collision many levels later. This is one prime source of disagreement in counts. ([Wikipedia][2])

3. **Top‑level equality reduces to an integer test—but with enormous inputs.**
   Two final values are equal iff their top exponents $Z_1,Z_2$ satisfy $Z_1-Z_2\in 4\mathbb Z$. Numerically you see $Z_1-Z_2$ to high precision and ask, “Is its real part *exactly* a multiple of 4 and its imaginary part *exactly* 0?” With numbers produced by long chains of $\pm\pi$, exponentials of $\pm\pi/2$, and many wrap integers, that becomes a certification problem: a tiny error in *any* interior wrap leaks into the final $Z$ by factors like $e^{\pm \pi/2}$, so you need provable bounds, not heuristics. (Heuristics yield counts like **2919** or **2926** depending on how near‑equal pairs are clustered.) ([OEIS][1])

4. **“Islands of associativity” cause structured collisions you must detect symbolically.**
   For example, at $n=4$ we really have

   $$
   i^{(i^i)^i} = ((i^i)^i)^i = e^{\pi/2},
   $$

   an *exact* identity coming from $\Log(i)=i\pi/2$ and $\Log(-i)=-i\pi/2$. At higher $n$, larger real subtrees can occur and create further exact coincidences. A purely numeric dedup can either miss these (overcount) or falsely merge near‑coincidences (undercount) unless these patterns are recognized and proved. ([OEIS][1])

5. **There is no known “A000081‑style” universal upper bound here.**
   For real positive bases $a>0$, the count of distinct values for $n$ copies is $\le$ the number of unlabeled rooted trees on $n$ nodes (A000081), essentially because the real laws $(x^y)^z=x^{yz}$ stabilize many shapes. With principal complex powers that identity simply fails (see the $\exp(-2\pi)$ example), and we cannot import those strong bounds or simplifications; the complex case is genuinely slipperier. ([OEIS][1])

6. **Status as of now:** the $n=12$ line on OEIS still reads “either 2919 or 2926,” left **unconfirmed** pending a proof‑quality computation. In other words, the obstacle isn’t producing *a* number—it’s producing a **certificate** that every wrap decision is correct and every equality/inequality is established. ([OEIS][1])

## How one would *certify* $a(12)$ (sketch)

A robust approach (and the kind of thing I’d implement in Mathematica or a big‑float library) is:

1. **Never evaluate values—evaluate logs.**
   Represent every intermediate as a pair $(\rho,\theta)$ with $\theta\in(-\pi,\pi]$.

2. **Propagate *intervals* with directed rounding.**
   Carry $(\rho,\theta)$ as narrow rational intervals; when forming $x^y$, compute
   $(a\rho-b\theta,\ a\theta+b\rho)$ interval‑wise, then choose the unique integer $k$ for which the *whole* phase interval lies inside $(-(2k+1)\pi,(2k+1)\pi]$. If it straddles a boundary, increase precision and recompute until it doesn’t.

3. **Record the wrap integers.**
   The list of all $k$’s for a tree is a discrete “signature.” If two trees share the same signature and the same final $(\rho,\theta)$ intervals, they are the same value.

4. **Top‑level dedup:** because the base is $i$, reduce final equality to checking $Z_1-Z_2\in 4\mathbb Z$ by interval arithmetic on $Z=$ (top exponent). If the interval for $\operatorname{Im}(Z_1-Z_2)$ excludes $0$, they’re distinct; if it contains $0$ and the interval for $\operatorname{Re}(Z_1-Z_2)/4$ contains an integer only, they are equal; otherwise refine precision.

This is algorithmically straightforward but numerically punishing at the handful of near‑boundary cases that determine whether $a(12)=2919$ or $2926$.

## Concrete small‑$n$ illustrations

* $n=3$:
  $(i^i)^i = i^{-1}=-i$, while $i^{(i^i)}=i^{e^{-\pi/2}}=\exp\!\big(i\tfrac{\pi}{2}e^{-\pi/2}\big)$, two distinct values—hence $a(3)=2$. ([OEIS][1])

* $n=4$:
  Five trees but only three values; in particular

  $$
  i^{(i^i)^i} = ((i^i)^i)^i = e^{\pi/2}\quad\text{and}\quad
  (i^i)^{(i^i)}=(i^{(i^i)})^i,
  $$

  explicit examples of the collapses discussed above. ([OEIS][1])

### Bottom line

The sequence asks a clean question—*How many genuinely different values can a power‑tower of $i$ take if you try every bracketing?*—whose answer hinges on subtle, discrete choices enforced by the principal branch of the logarithm. By $n=12$ the computation is no longer about CPU time; it’s about **certifying** a handful of wrap decisions and equalities amidst numbers living at incomprehensible scale. That’s why $a(12)$ has remained a tantalizing line in the OEIS for years. ([OEIS][1])
